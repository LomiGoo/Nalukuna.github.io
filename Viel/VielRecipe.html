Sisig
  • 1 lb pork belly
  • 1/2 lb pork ears
  • 1/2 lb pork liver
  • 1/4 cup vinegar
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 1 onion (finely chopped)
  • 3 cloves garlic (minced)
  • 2-3 Thai chilies (finely chopped)
  • 1-2 tablespoons cooking oil
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • 1 teaspoon black pepper
  • 1 tablespoon calamansi juice or lemon juice
  • 1 egg (optional)
  1. Boil pork belly, pork ears, and pork liver separately until tender. Chop into small pieces. Pork ears add a distinctive texture.
  1. Traditionally, the boiled meats are grilled to add a smoky flavor. Grill the pork belly, ears, and liver until they have a slight char. Chop into small, bite-sized pieces.
  1. Heat oil in a pan over medium heat. Sauté garlic and onion until fragrant.
  1. Add the chopped grilled meats to the pan. Stir-fry until crispy.
  1. Pour in vinegar and soy sauce. Cook until the vinegar smell evaporates. Add salt and pepper to taste.
  1. Stir in chopped chilies and calamansi or lemon juice. Cook for another 2-3 minutes.
  1. If you like, you can add a raw egg to the hot sisig and stir until it’s cooked through, or just serve it on top.
  1. Transfer to a sizzling plate or serving dish and serve hot with rice.
Adobo
  • 2 lbs chicken or pork, cut into pieces
  • 1/2 cup soy sauce
  • 1/4 cup vinegar
  • 1/2 cup water
  • 1 head garlic, minced
  • 2 bay leaves
  • 1 tsp black peppercorns
  • 1 tbsp sugar (optional)
  • 2 tbsp cooking oil
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  1. In a bowl, marinate the meat in soy sauce, garlic, and bay leaves for at least 30 minutes.
  1. Heat oil in a pan over medium heat. Remove meat from marinade and brown it on all sides.
  1. Add the marinade, vinegar, water, and peppercorns to the pan. Bring to a boil.
  1. Reduce heat, cover, and simmer for 30-40 minutes or until the meat is tender.
  1. Adjust seasoning with salt, pepper, and sugar if desired. Simmer uncovered until the sauce thickens.
  1. Serve with rice.
Sinigang
  • 2 lbs pork (ribs or belly), cut into pieces
  • 1 large onion, quartered
  • 2-3 tomatoes, quartered
  • 1 daikon radish (labanos), sliced
  • 1 cup string beans, cut into pieces
  • 1 medium eggplant, cut into chunks
  • 1-2 green chili peppers (optional)
  • 1 packet tamarind soup mix (or about 1/2 cup fresh tamarind juice)
  • 8 cups water
  • 2 tbsp fish sauce (patis)
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 1 cup kangkong (water spinach) or other leafy greens
  1. Boil pork in water until tender, about 45 minutes to 1 hour.
  1. Add onions and tomatoes, cooking until softened.
  1. Stir in tamarind soup mix or tamarind juice. Adjust sourness as desired.
  1. Add radish, cooking until tender.
  1. Add eggplant and string beans, simmer until vegetables are cooked.
  1. Season with fish sauce, salt, and pepper. Add chili peppers if desired.
  1. Stir in leafy greens and cook until wilted.
  1. Serve hot with rice.
Tinola
  • 1 lb chicken (cut into pieces)
  • 1 tablespoon vegetable oil
  • 4 cloves garlic (minced)
  • 1 thumb-sized piece of ginger (sliced)
  • 2 cups water or chicken broth
  • 1 cup green papaya or chayote (peeled and sliced)
  • 1 cup spinach or malunggay leaves (substitute with kale if unavailable)
  • 2 tablespoons fish sauce
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  1. In a large pot, heat the vegetable oil over medium heat.
  1. Add the onions, garlic, and ginger. Sauté until the onions are translucent and the mixture is fragrant.
  1. Cook Chicken: Add the chicken pieces and cook until they are lightly browned on all sides.
  1. Pour in the water or chicken broth and bring to a boil. Once boiling, reduce the heat to a simmer.
  1. Let it simmer for about 20-30 minutes, or until the chicken is cooked through and tender.
  1. Add the papaya or chayote and cook until tender, about 5-10 minutes.
  1. Stir in the fish sauce, and season with salt and pepper to taste.
  1. Add the spinach or malunggay leaves and cook for an additional 2-3 minutes until the greens are wilted.
  1. Serve with rice
Lumpiang Shanghai
  • 1 pound ground pork (or chicken, if preferred)
  • 1 cup finely chopped carrots
  • 1/2 cup finely chopped onions
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1/2 cup finely chopped green onions
  • 1/2 cup water chestnuts, finely chopped (optional)
  • 1/2 cup soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon oyster sauce (optional)
  • 1 teaspoon ground black pepper
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1 egg, beaten (for binding)
  1. Lumpia wrappers
  1. Oil for frying
  1. In a large bowl, combine the ground pork, carrots, onions, garlic, green onions, and water chestnuts (if using).
  1. Add soy sauce, oyster sauce (if using), black pepper, salt, and the beaten egg. Mix thoroughly until all ingredients are well combined.
  1. Place a lumpia wrapper on a clean surface, with one corner pointing towards you (like a diamond shape).
  1. Spoon about 1 to 2 tablespoons of filling near the bottom corner of the wrapper.
  1. Fold the bottom corner over the filling, then fold the sides inwards.
  1. Roll the wrapper tightly but not too tight, and seal the edge with a little water or flour paste (flour mixed with a bit of water).
  1. Heat oil in a deep frying pan or wok over medium heat.
  1. Carefully add the lumpia, a few at a time, without overcrowding.
  1. Fry until golden brown and crispy, about 3-5 minutes, turning occasionally to ensure even cooking.
  1. Remove and drain on paper towels.
  1. Serve hot with a dipping sauce such as sweet and sour sauce, vinegar with garlic, or a simple soy sauce with calamansi.
